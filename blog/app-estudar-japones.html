Why Japanese Is Hard (and Why Most People Quit)

Japanese is one of the most challenging languages for English speakers. The U.S. Foreign Service Institute estimates it takes 2,200 class hours to reach professional proficiency. Three writing systems (hiragana, katakana, kanji), complex grammar, and thousands of characters make it easy to feel overwhelmed.

Most learners quit within the first 6 months. The reason? They forget faster than they learn. Without a system to retain what you study, you're constantly re-learning the same kanji, the same vocabulary, the same grammar patterns.

Spaced repetition solves this problem. Instead of letting knowledge fade, it schedules reviews at the exact moment you're about to forget, turning short-term memories into permanent ones.

The 3 Pillars of Japanese Study

An effective Japanese study plan covers three main areas, and each benefits enormously from flashcards and spaced repetition:

1. Writing Systems: Hiragana, Katakana & Kanji

Hiragana (46 characters) and katakana (46 characters) can be learned in 1-2 weeks with dedicated flashcard practice. Create one card per character with the symbol on the front and the romanized reading on the back.

Kanji is the real challenge. There are 2,136 jouyou kanji (commonly used characters) that Japanese students learn by the end of high school. Each kanji has multiple readings (on'yomi and kun'yomi) and meanings. Without spaced repetition, memorizing even 500 kanji is nearly impossible.

Tips for Effective Japanese Study

  1. Learn hiragana and katakana first before touching kanji. Use flashcards for 1-2 weeks until you can read them instantly.
  2. Don't study kanji in isolation. Always learn kanji with vocabulary words that use them.
  3. Use example sentences. Japanese words often have different nuances depending on context. Include sentences in your flashcards.
  4. Review daily, even if just 10 minutes. Spaced repetition only works with consistency.
  5. Start with the most frequent words. The 1,000 most common words cover ~80% of everyday Japanese.
  6. Combine passive and active study. Use Mayinab for active recall, but also watch anime, read manga, or listen to podcasts for immersion.
  7. Don't add too many new cards. 10-15 new cards per day is sustainable. 50+ will cause review pile-up.
