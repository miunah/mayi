How to Make Study Summaries That Actually Help You Remember

The Problem with Most Summaries

Most students spend hours making beautiful summaries they never review. Color-coded notes, perfectly organized pages, meticulous highlighting ‚Äî and then the notebook sits on a shelf until exam day.

The act of writing a summary is somewhat useful for understanding, but a summary you don't actively review is a summary you'll forget. The real question isn't "how do I make a pretty summary?" ‚Äî it's "how do I make a summary that turns into long-term memory?"

The answer: make summaries that become flashcards.

The 6 Rules of Effective Summaries

1. Use Your Own Words

Copying text from a book or presentation is transcription, not summarizing. When you rephrase information in your own words, you're forced to actually understand it. This processing is where the initial learning happens.

Bad: "The mitochondria are membrane-bound organelles found in the cytoplasm of eukaryotic cells that generate most of the cell's supply of adenosine triphosphate (ATP)."

Good: "Mitochondria = the 'power plant' of the cell. Makes ATP (energy). Found inside all cells with a nucleus."

2. Focus on Key Concepts, Not Details

A summary that's as long as the original material isn't a summary ‚Äî it's a copy. Identify the 20% of information that captures 80% of the meaning.

Ask yourself:

  • What is the main idea of this section?
  • What would appear on an exam question?
  • If I could only remember one thing from this page, what would it be?

3. Structure with Clear Headings

Good summaries have a clear hierarchy: main topic ‚Üí subtopics ‚Üí key points. This structure mirrors how your brain organizes information and makes it easier to navigate later.

In Mayinab, you can use Markdown headers (# Topic, ## Subtopic) to create structured notes that are easy to scan.

4. Include Examples and Connections

Abstract concepts are hard to remember. Concrete examples and connections to prior knowledge make them stick. After stating a definition, add a brief example or analogy.

Bare: "Osmosis: movement of water through semipermeable membrane."

Better: "Osmosis: water moves from where it's abundant to where it's scarce, through a membrane. Example: pruny fingers in the bath (water moves into your skin cells)."

5. Keep It Concise

Your summary should be fast to re-read. If it takes 30 minutes to review a summary, it's too long. Aim for:

  • 1 page per chapter (or per major topic)
  • Bullet points over paragraphs
  • Abbreviations you understand
  • Tables for comparisons

6. Turn Key Points into Flashcards

This is the most important step ‚Äî and the one most students skip. After writing your summary, go through it and convert each key point into a flashcard.

Popular Summary Methods

Cornell Method

Divide the page into 3 sections: cues (left), notes (right), and summary (bottom). The left column contains questions, the right has answers/notes. Cover the right side to test yourself ‚Äî instant active recall.

Outline Method

Use indented bullet points to create a hierarchical structure. Main ideas are at the top level, supporting details underneath. Fast to write and easy to scan.

Charting Method

Use tables to organize information that involves comparisons or categories. Perfect for comparing historical periods, types of cells, legal concepts, or anything with parallel attributes.

Flow Method

Write ideas freely and draw connections between them with arrows. Similar to mind maps but less structured. Good for brainstorming and exploring relationships between concepts.

Common Summary Mistakes

Mistake 1: Copying Instead of Summarizing

If your summary looks like the original text with different colors, you didn't summarize ‚Äî you transcribed. Close the book and write what you remember first, then check for accuracy.

Mistake 2: Making It Too Long

A 10-page summary of a 12-page chapter defeats the purpose. Be ruthless about what makes the cut. If it won't appear on an exam, it probably doesn't need to be in your summary.

Mistake 3: Never Reviewing the Summary

A summary you write and never look at again provides minimal benefit. The solution: convert it into flashcards so that spaced repetition forces you to actively review the content.

Mistake 4: Spending More Time Formatting Than Learning

Perfect color-coding, fancy headers, and aesthetic layouts feel productive but don't help memory. Spend your time on content quality, not visual polish. A plain-text summary with good flashcards beats a gorgeous one without.
